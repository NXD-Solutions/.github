# Test: Every element earns its place by serving the reader's current position in the document

## What the principle requires

Every element in a communicative artefact must pass two tests: it belongs, and the reader is ready for it at the point it appears.

## Observable behaviours

### Sequencing

- An example that references a concept not yet introduced is a failure — the example must follow the claim it proves.
- A non-obvious concept introduced before its prerequisite vocabulary has been established is a failure.
- Elements are ordered by what the reader needs next, not by the order the writer's reasoning produced them.

### Bridging

- A section transition that opens without connecting to the previous section is a failure.
- Every section boundary carries a sentence that closes what came before and earns what comes next.

### Signposting

- Prose that announces a contrast ("In contrast to X, Y does Z") instead of letting the contrast emerge from the sequence is a signal of sequencing failure.
- The fix is to rebuild the sequence so the contrast lands without announcement — not to remove the signpost and leave the gap.

## Boundary conditions

- "Write for the widest audience first" governs audience layering (who can stop reading). This principle governs sequential readiness within a layer. Both concern order; they do not overlap.
- "Reader-need filter" governs whether content belongs at all. This principle governs whether content that belongs is placed where the reader can receive it.
- Presentation choices (e.g. splitting principle and example onto separate lines for scan-ability) are not sequencing failures — they serve a different reader need.
