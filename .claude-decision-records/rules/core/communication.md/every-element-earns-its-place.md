# Every element earns its place by serving the reader's current position in the document

**Sub-principle of:** Every communicative artefact serves the reader's context — not the system's structure
**Strand:** Transparent by Nature
**Domain:** Culture (cross-cutting)
**Date:** 2026-03-29

---

## Trigger

Four distinct corrections during the authoring of "Write for the reader, not the system" shared one root: something placed before the reader was ready for it, or a gap left where a bridge should be. The pattern is positional — not qualitative in the abstract.

## What was decided

Add a sub-principle under "Every communicative artefact serves the reader's context" with three derived rules: Sequencing, Bridging, and Signposting. Placed in `communication.md` alongside the parent principle's existing rules.

## Two-test framing

The principle carries two tests: does this element belong, and is the reader ready for it? These are independent — an element can pass one and fail the other. Both must pass. This framing was confirmed as the clearest articulation and kept verbatim from the draft.

## Children: four collapsed to three

The Change Request identified four children from the source corrections:

1. Do not example what has not yet been visible
2. Hold the reveal of non-obvious concepts until the reader has the vocabulary to receive them
3. Bridge sections with a sentence that closes what came before and earns what comes next
4. Remove signposting that names a contrast instead of letting it land

Children 1 and 2 collapsed into the Sequencing rule — both are instances of presenting what the reader cannot yet receive. The Sequencing rule body makes the evidence/example case explicit ("An example proves a claim the reader has already seen — placed before the claim, it proves nothing") so the original child is recoverable.

What was ruled out: splitting principle and example onto separate lines was excluded — it is a presentation choice, not a sequencing failure.

## Signposting as positional failure

Signposting that names a contrast instead of letting it land was confirmed as a positional failure, not a prose quality failure. The argument: signposting is evidence of a sequencing failure — if the contrast were earned by what came before it, the announcement would be redundant. The signpost appears because the writer knows the contrast is coming but hasn't built the ground for it to land on.

## Boundary with "Write for the widest audience first"

Both principles concern order. They do not overlap:

- "Write for the widest audience first" governs audience layering — who can stop reading and still leave informed.
- "Every element earns its place by serving the reader's current position" governs sequential readiness — whether the reader is ready for what comes next within a layer.

The pair test (P1) passed cleanly. This boundary is documented because the adjacency is close enough to warrant an explicit record.
